Two Women with a Different Background Fighting for the Same Causes: The Abolition of Slavery and Women's rights

May 6, 2021
7 min read

Women played a significant role in the fight against slavery and racial injustice. Angelina Grimke, a southerner aristocrat and Sojourner Truth, a former slave were both prominent figures in the 19th century. Despite their differences, both fought for the same issues; racial and gender inequality.


Background


Angelina Grimke

Angelina Grimke Weld was born on February 20, 1805, in Charleston, South Carolina to a wealthy slave-holding family. Angelina and her sister Sarah grew up witnessing the horror of slavery from an early age. By the time they reached 20 years old, they could no longer bear living with the practice of slavery and left the south to move to Philadelphia. They became deeply involved in the abolition movement and joined the Philadelphia Female Anti-Slavery Society. Angelina became one of the first women to speak publicly in front of both men and women on the issue of slavery. An act considered scandalous at the time.

In 1836, Angelina Grimke wrote a unique piece; An Appeal to the Christians Women of the South. This pamphlet was significant at the time because it was written by a woman and addressed to other women with the hope to persuade on the evil of slavery. While her pamphlet was well received in the North among the abolitionists, it was burned publicly in the south and the Grimke sisters were banned to visit Charleston ever again. Women had no place in the political scene at the time, they were expected to stay home, and their opinions were not welcomed. In her pamphlet, Grimke encouraged women to breaks those standards, to speak up and act in any way they could to get involved in the abolition of slavery.

Angelina Grimke

Sojourner Truth

Sojourner Truth was an African American born into slavery in 1797 in Ulster County, New York, under the name of Isabella Baumfree. Unlike Angelina Grimke, Truth’s early life was marked by harsh physical labor. She was sold and bought several times. Truth’s journey to freedom began in 1826, a year before New York state law to emancipate slaves took effect. She escaped with her daughter Sophia but was forced to leave her other children behind. In 1828, Truth moved to New York and learned that her five-years-old son has been sold illegally into slavery in Alabama. She decided to take legal action to rescue him and was the first black woman to sue a white man, and won the case. It was during the year 1843, that Truth’s life took a turning point. She became a preacher and involved in the abolition movement in which she met William Lloyd Garrison and Frederick Douglass. While Truth dedicated her time speaking on racial inequality, she was also an activist for women’s rights.

Sojourner Truth never learned how to read and write, however, she became a great orator and well regarded for her powerful speeches. One of the most famous speeches of all time, known as “Ain’t I a Women” was delivered in 1851 at the Ohio Women’s Rights Convention with the purpose to advocate for women’s rights. Truth constructed her speech based on her personal experience as a former slave as well as her knowledge of the Bible. Filled with empowering words, her speech impacted thousands of people across the country.

Their similarities and differences


Speaking in public in the 19th century as a woman

Grimke and Truth were both strong and passionate women who stood up for what they believed in and that was equal rights for everyone, including men, women, and slaves. The main purpose of An Appeal to the Christian Women of the South was to inspire as well as to persuade the audience on the evil of slavery. She does not directly advocate for women’s rights but her act of speaking on such a matter was a way to show that women had the right to be involved in a cause they felt strongly about and share their opinions. While most women’s rights activists supported abolition, not all abolitionists supported women’s rights. A perfect example of this is Catharine Beecher who exchanged a series of letters with Grimke discussing her views and involvement as a woman in the abolitionist cause. Beecher had a very contrasting view of the role of women in society. She believed that women were subordinate, and their role was restricted to the domestic sphere rather than politics. The constant confrontation and discrimination Grmke received when speaking in public led her to realize the inequality between men and women and turned her attention toward women’s rights.

What is interesting in Grimke’s pamphlet is that she recognizes women don’t possess any sort of influence in the political scene. She tells her audience to use their submissive position in the household to persuade the men around them responsible for making the decisions. She says “I know you do not make the laws, but I also know that you are the wives and mothers, the sisters and daughters of the ones who do”. Sojourner Truth, on the other hand, refutes the idea that women and men were different. She began her speech by making a bold statement by establishing herself as a woman in front of her audience, she declares " I am women’s right“. In the first part of the speech, Truth argues that women are physically equal to men and that this should not be determined by the body but rather by the work they could do. She uses her personal experience as a former slave to depicts the hard physical work she had done in the fields and says,”I have as much muscle as any man and can do as much work as any man. I have plowed and reaped and husked and chopped and mowed and can any man do more than that?".
